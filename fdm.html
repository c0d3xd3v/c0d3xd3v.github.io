Finite Differenzen Methode¶

finite differences

the taylor expension at a point $a$ is given by

\begin{equation} \nonumber Tf(x;a) = ‎‎\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \end{equation} \begin{equation} \nonumber f(x) = Tf(x;x-\Delta x) = ‎‎\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(x-\Delta x)}{n!}(x-(x-\Delta x))^n \end{equation} \begin{equation} \nonumber f(x) = ‎‎\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(x-\Delta x)}{n!}\Delta x^n \end{equation}

first derivative

we can split this series after the first $k$ terms such that

\begin{equation} \nonumber f(x) = f(x-\Delta x) + f'(x-\Delta x)\Delta x + ... + r(x) \end{equation}

where $r(x)$ defines the error of order $\mathcal{O}(\Delta x^{k+1})$ if we truncate the series after $k$ terms

\begin{equation} \nonumber r(x) = ‎‎\sum_{n=k+1}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(x-\Delta x)}{n!}\Delta x^n \end{equation}

with this we can get approximations for the first darivative in terms of the taylor expension

\begin{eqnarray} \nonumber f'(x-\Delta x) &=& \frac{f(x) - f(x-\Delta x)}{\Delta x}\\ \nonumber \Rightarrow f'(x) &=& \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \end{eqnarray}

called forward difference and setting $\Delta x := -\Delta x$ backward differences

\begin{eqnarray} \nonumber f'(x+\Delta x) &=& -\frac{f(x) - f(x+\Delta x)}{\Delta x}\\ \nonumber \Rightarrow f'(x) &=& \frac{ f(x)-f(x - \Delta x)}{\Delta x} \end{eqnarray}

the error term then is

\begin{equation} \nonumber r(x) = \frac{ f''(x)\Delta x^2}{2\Delta x} + ... =: \mathcal{O}(\Delta x) \end{equation} building the avarage of forward $f_\underrightarrow{\Delta}$ and backward $f_\underleftarrow{\Delta}$ diffrences results in the so called central diffrence quotient \begin{equation} \nonumber f_{\Delta_c} = \frac{f_\underrightarrow{\Delta} + f_\underleftarrow{\Delta}}{2}=\frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x - \Delta x)}{2\Delta x} \end{equation}

for the error estimation we notice that the terms of the second derivative is chanceled by subtraction and we get

\begin{equation} r(x) = 2\frac{f^{(3)}(x)}{3!\Delta x}\Delta x^3 + ... =: \mathcal{O}(\Delta x^2) \end{equation}

numerical experiments



second order derivative


Using the taylor series \begin{equation} \nonumber f(x) = Tf(x;x-\Delta x) \end{equation} and truncation after the term containing the second derivative we get \begin{equation} f(x + \Delta x) = f(x) + f'(x)\Delta x + \frac{1}{2} f''(x)\Delta x^2 + ... \\ \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} f''(x)\Delta x^2 = f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) - f'(x)\Delta x \end{equation} The same procedure can be used with \begin{equation} \nonumber f(x) = Tf(x;x + \Delta x) \end{equation} to obtain \begin{equation} f(x - \Delta x) = f(x) - f'(x)\Delta x + \frac{1}{2} f''(x)\Delta x^2 + ... \\ \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} f''(x)\Delta x^2 = f(x - \Delta x) - f(x) + f'(x)\Delta x \end{equation} This are the forward and backward differences. By adding this two equations gives the second order derivative approximation \begin{equation} f''(x) = \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - 2f(x) + f(x - \Delta x)}{\Delta x^2} \end{equation} for the error we notice that the term containing $f'''(x)$ is chanceled out and we get \begin{equation} r(x) = 2\frac{f^{(4)}(x)}{4!\Delta x^2}\Delta x^4 + ... =: \mathcal{O}(\Delta x^2) \end{equation}
